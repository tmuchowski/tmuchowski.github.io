Is Chinese AI about to burst the American tech bubble? 🇨🇳🇺🇸

July 8, 2026 | Translations:
  • Pl

While Silicon Valley spends billions developing increasingly powerful models, China has found a way to offer 90% of the same quality at a fraction of the cost. How is this possible? Here are the key takeaways from the analysis of the current AI market situation published at https://lnkd.in/dEc2j9wG :

🚀 Extreme cost disparity The US spends around $1 trillion annually on building AI (3% of its GDP), while China’s spending is just a fraction of that – approx. $102-123 billion (0.6% of GDP). The result? The exact same coding task that costs $2.33 in America’s Claude Opus costs just $0.31 in China’s GLM model – that’s 7.5 times cheaper!

🧠 The “copying homework” strategy Chinese companies use a method called distillation. Instead of training models from scratch for billions of dollars, they train their systems using responses generated by the most expensive US models. In practice, this means that every dollar spent by Americans on foundational research becomes a sort of “donation” to the Chinese AI industry, which compresses those results into smaller, cheaper models.

💼 “Good enough” AI for business Although the US still holds the title for the “smartest” models in the world, Chinese alternatives (DeepSeek, Qwen, GLM) dominate the middle of global leaderboards. For 90% of companies that need AI for customer service or routine office tasks, the most expensive model isn’t necessary – a much cheaper one is more than enough.

🛡️ The trust and sovereignty issue The US is beginning to restrict access to its technologies (e.g., cutting off foreign users from Claude), forcing countries like France, Germany, or the UK to look for alternatives they can control. This opens the door for cheaper and more open models from China. The takeaway? The market is starting to notice that the “pay-per-token” business model, which generates losses with every query, might be unsustainable against efficient and cheap competition from the East. Are we heading toward a “reckoning moment” for Wall Street investors?
